Abstract Algebra, Part 1

November 6, 2013

The concepts of abstract algebra are so pervasive. They tend to crop up everwhere in computer science. Functional programming, coding theory1 and cryptography are some examples of areas which rely heavily on abstract algebraic concpets.

Since my knowledge of these concepts is so sketchy, I found myself incapable of grasping many of the ideas behind functional programming constructs like Monads, Functors and Applicatives. After all, you can’t hope to understand what a Monad really is (apart from the futile bind/return definition), without learning at least the basics of category theory. An obvious prerequisite to category theory is abstract algebra.

I sought to put an end to this agony by setting aside some time to study abstract algebra and category theory. I decided to start by watching Benedict Gross abstract algebra class lectures from Harvard. In parallel I’m planning to read the book he recommended for the subject, called Algebra, by Michael Artin.

I’ll be writing a series of posts to document the ideas I find most interesting in every chapter. This article reviews chapters 1 & 2.

  1. See chapter 8 of “Abstract Algebra, Theory and Applications” by Thomas Judson.↩
