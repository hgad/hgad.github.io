How to Read a Book

November 7, 2013

Levels of Reading

  1. Elementary Reading,
  2. Inspectional Reading,
  3. Analytical Reading,
  4. Syntopical Reading.

Stages of Reading

The author breaks every level of reading into stages as follows.

Elementary Reading

  • Reading Readiness,
  • Word Mastery,
  • Vocabulary Growth and the Utilization of Context,
  • Functional Literacy.

The author speaks of elementary reading as the physical ability to read, which is usually taught in early years of school, however, I think it should refer to learning the vocabulary and terminology of the subject the book is about before actually reading the book.

Inspectional Reading

  • Systematic Skimming:
    • Read the front page and preface,
    • Read the table of contents thoroughly,
    • Read the index and checkout the terms you feel important,
    • Read the publisher blurb unless you think it’s complete puffery, which also gives an idea of whether the book really has something to say about the subject.
  • Superficial Reading:
    • Skim through the pages of the book reading lines, paragraphs or couple pages here and there, but no more than that. Don’t worry about terms you don’t understand at this point (this refutes my thought about elementary reading). Particularly important are the last few pages of a book, because authors tend to summarize their message in these pages.

Inspectional reading is enough to decide whether or not you want to pursue the knowledge in this book.

How to be a Demanding Reader?

By asking these questions:

  1. What’s the book about?
  2. What are the main points the author wishes to convey?
  3. Is he true about them?
  4. What significance does this hold to me as a seeker of knowledge?

The first two questions can usually be answered during inspectional reading. The last two questions require analytical reading and should be continuously asked for every argument formulated by the author.

Analytical Reading

  1. Categorize (or pigeonhole as the author likes to call it) a book during inspectional reading: Fictional vs. Expository, Theoretical vs. Practical, and if theoretical, whether it’s philosophical or scientific. The author argues that philosophical books draw conclusions mostly from day-to-day experiences, while scientific books usually rely on lab-based observations.

  2. State the unity (the plot or the plan) of the book in a single sentence or at most a few sentences (a short paragraph).

  3. Set forth the major parts of the book and show how these are organized into a whole, by being ordered to one another and to the unity of the whole.

  4. Find out what the author’s problems were.

  5. Come to terms with the author: Find important terms the author uses, and for every term, make sure you understand the meaning the author intended to convey.
